Introduction to Testing

Christine Julien, The University of Texas at Austin

 

In software, testing is the process of finding bugs. It is a dynamic approach, which refers to the fact that we run the code for some inputs and check that the outputs are correct. This only checks correctness for some executions.

Debugging is the process of removing bugs; it includes both locating and removing bugs. Debugging is not the same thing as testing.

There are three key components of a testing process:

  • generating the test inputs
  • determining the adequacy of the test suite (coverage)
  • creating test oracles (basically whether a test should pass or fail)

Some other terminology (though these terms are not always used consistently):

  • a fault is one or more incorrect lines of code
  • an error is an incorrect (unobserved) state caused by a fault
  • a failure is in incorrect (observed) state caused by a fault

The goal of testing is to make both errors and failures observable.

The first use of the term "bug" is attributed to Thomas Edison (1878):

"It has been just so in all of my inventions. The first step is an intuition, and comes with a burst, then difficulties arise--this thing gives out and [it is] then that "Bugs"--as such little faults and difficulties are called--show themselves and months of intense watching, study and labor are requisite before commercial success or failure is certainly resolved."

When people think of the first "bug" in a computer program, they often think of the incident in 1947, when researchers at Harvard were working on the early Mark II computer and found a moth actually trapped in a relay in the computer. In any case, we now routinely use "bug" and "debug" as part of the language of computing.

There are many many cases of software failures that were catastrophic in one way or another. This is a great article highlighting some of them. My quick summary of a few:

  • inconsistent unit of measure (one team used English units another metric) leads to crash of Mars orbital probe
  • arithmetic overflow (trying to smash a 64 bit number into 16 bits) leads to crash of Ariane launcher
  • a (known) problem with clock drift caused a failure in the Patriot missile, leading to the deaths of (at least) 28 US soldiers
  • a race condition in a radiation therapy machine delivers overdose of radiation, sickening at least six people and eventually killing some
  • a Soviet early-warning missile system full of software bugs issues faulty warning of five inbound nuclear missiles. (Luckily, the Lt. Col. in charge took a risk and assumed they were faults, thus preventing a nuclear war.)

A 2002 study found that software bugs cost almost $60B annually.

Types of Testing

Testing occurs (or should occur) at many stages in the lifecycle of a software system.

Unit testing is the most common and easiest to understand/do. Unit testing refers to the verification of individual methods (or similar components) in a software system. (This is what we'll do in our tutorial.)

Integration testing is the verification of the interactions among multiple modules in the system.

System testing is the testing of the system as a whole.

Acceptance testing is the validation of the software against the user requirements.

Performance testing refers to the characterization of the performance of the software.

Regression testing is more a style of testing that refers to testing new versions of the software against old tests.

Conclusion

Testing itself is a whole sub-domain of software engineering, and we won't get into all of the details. In general, there are lots and lots of tools available to help you test your programs adequately, regardless of the platform or language. Test-driven development is a style of writing software that promotes writing the tests first, based on what you want to achieve. The general point, though, is to not dismiss testing as a necessary element of software engineering.
